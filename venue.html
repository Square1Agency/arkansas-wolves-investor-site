01 / Venue

The home of Arkansas soccer
starts here.

In every investor conversation, the venue is the first real question. Where it sits, what it costs, who pays for it, and what it does the other 47 weeks of the year. This is our answer.

The Argument

Without a venue, there is no club. Without a venue, the path to profitability — already long in lower-division professional soccer — becomes impossible. Ticket sales, parking, concessions, merchandise, sponsor placement, broadcast revenue, non-soccer events: every revenue stream a Wolves ownership group can pull on flows through one physical asset.

18 home soccer games. 52 weeks of programming. The venue is the financial engine of this club. The venue is the brand. The venue is, in real terms, the reason Little Rock will have professional soccer at all.

Proof Points

It's been built — three times recently.

Phoenix Rising

27 Days · $7.5M

Ron led this build personally. Stadium designed and standing in under a month, on a tight budget, on the side of the most-used freeway in Phoenix. The best billboard money could've bought.

Rhode Island FC

Riverfront Mixed-Use

Providence built a riverfront stadium on a five-acre parcel, with the surrounding three acres developed as hotel, restaurants, and shopping. The closest direct analog to what Little Rock can become.

Sacramento Republic

30,000 Seat Opening Night

From idea to opening match in front of 30,000 in USL Championship play. Warren Smith (now consulting Ozark FC) is the playbook reference for getting from blueprint to fan in seats.

The Long View

A full club facility, not just a stadium.

The European club model puts everything in one place. On a 4–6 acre parcel: the stadium itself, locker rooms and offices, practice fields, a youth academy complex, and a fitness facility with memberships sold to parents while their kids train. Every day of the year, something is happening on this land. Every dollar of overhead is spread across more revenue than just home matches.

"How awesome would that be? Parents drop kids at practice, buy a gym membership, work out for an hour and a half — done by the time practice ends."

— Ron Gilmore
